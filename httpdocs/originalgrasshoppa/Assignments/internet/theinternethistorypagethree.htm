The Internet quickly grew and encompassed any country in the world with a phone line or cable that linked to another computer of another country. Where computers and phones went, the internet went.

        But the internet started out slow. The first message ever sent on the internet was "lo" in 1969. The original word that was planned to be sent at a, then, wopping 50 kb/s was "login", but as the sent it the computer crashed allowing only "lo" through.

    Within three months The University of Utah and University of California Santa Barbara both had their own ARPANET nodes making a total of four in the world.

       By the end of 1970 there had been an addition of six nodes and nineteen host computers (a computer that provides end users with services such as database access).

    In 1971 Ray Tomlinson created the first email program. It was called CPYNET. In order to extend the range of people who could use the program to any one in the network he created the "commercial at" symbol to combine the user and host names. We still use the symbol today in our emails (australianchicken@thedinnertable.com).

    Then in 1973 the ARPANET makes an enormous leap, Hawaii joins the ARPANET via it's satellite link. This allowed computers to connect with out a cable by sending electrical impulses through the air.

    In 1974 access to the ARPANET was given to commercial companies with a program created by Bolt - Beranek & Newman (BBN). This program was called Telnet.

    In 1977 the ARPANET has a boom as it hits 111 hosts.

    In 1980 the ARPANET was downed by a virus.

    In 1982 the ARPANET was back and kicking. As a result Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) and Internet Protocol (IP) established a system by which different networks can communicate. These linked networks come to be known as the internet.

    In 1984 the internet reaches an astounding 1,000 hosts world wide.

    In 1985 Canada had a network of connections for their universities called the the NetNorth.

    During 1988 Canada joins NSFNET, an international backbone of computing centres that enables more network connections.

    And just when you thought this had nothing to do with the internet today, BAM~~the World Wide Web (WWW) is created by Tim Berners-Lee of the European Organization for Nuclear Research (CERN) and there are 100,000 internet hosts worldwide.

    In all the excitement of 1990 ARPANET ceased it's functions and gave way to the internet. Tim Berners-Lee then authored the first browser-editor, called WorldWideWeb. He also authored the communication language of the internet - Hypertext Transfer Protocol (HTTP) as well as the standard by which web pages were to be written, known as Hypertext Markup Language (HTML).

    In 1992 a revolution was started as the first video and audio were sent across the internet which had hit one million hosts.

    In 1993 this was reported to be as much fact as the air we breathe is not pure oxygen. There are 15 million people online worldwide and the CBC's Bill Cameron reported, "The internet is growing like an embryonic brain at a rate of 10 per cent a month."

    Late 1994 Netscape launched the first available beta version of the predominate commercial web browser. this new browser was called Mozilla 0.96b.

    In  1995 the internet completed it's world wide infection with Sun Microsystems introduction of Java programming language, RealAudio's introduction, which allowed users to listen to audio over the internet in near-real time. Then CBC goes online with its website, and to cap it all off the Vatican goes online with its home page.

    In September of 1998 the user's dreams and prayers were answered with the creation of the first uncensored, unfiltered, and efficient search engine, Google.

    In 2002 it was announced that there are more than two million internet hosts and 840 million users worldwide

    According to http://www.internetworldstats.com/ the number of people using the internet by the end of 2005 was 1,018,057,389.

    The internet continues to grow rapidly today. More and more countries get more and more populated and more people in different parts of the world buy a computer with internet access.

 

As you can see the internet had a long and winding rise to power. What we take for granted is a complicated network of computers that were created as a sideaffect of an experiment of the government and universities around the world.
